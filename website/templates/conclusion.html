Conclusion

Word vectorization allows a historian to survey textual sources at a volume that would be unimaginable without the assistance of a computer. Comparing the similarity of words over time can guide a researcher to identify and interrogate trends over time at a macro scale.

However, these indications are insufficient on their own. Due to the black box nature of neural networks, what each dimension of a word vector represents, and thus the reasons why words are semantically similar, cannot be determined. Nor can the vector model determine what parts of the corpus led it to make certain associations between words. In order to truly understand what a set of word vectors is showing, a historian needs to further analyze the underlying sources and develop their supporting evidence from there. Word vectorization can serve as a tool during research; it is not, without further work, sufficient to draw sound conclusions.

There are clear avenues for expansion and improvement on the specific visualizations made here. Most obviously, the size of the corpuses can be increased. While Chronicling America does represent newspapers from across the country, it is heavily concentrated on a couple of publications. Other online archives, like the one for the New York Times, could be incorporated in order to create a more holistic picture of American public opinion. The distribution of pages per publication could also be altered to ensure no one newspaper dominates the resulting model

As well, in the interest of keeping the project lean and efficient, only pages with text that explicitly mentions “Stalin” were included in the corpuses. This limits the potential comparison of word vectors, as associations for words other than “Stalin” are skewed towards the prominence of “Stalin” in the text. Word vectorization could be applied to a much larger set of newspaper articles across these time periods. While the resulting models would take longer to train and require much more storage, this would allow the analysis of any number of words that appear in the corpus, rather than just focusing on one like “Stalin.” A tool could be built to allow the user to explore these word vectors and their associations at their own pace.
